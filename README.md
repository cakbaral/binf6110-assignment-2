## Introduction
*Saccharomyces cerevisiae* is one of, if not, the most recognizable species of yeast that has been widely used for a variety of industrial applications (Maneira et al., 2025, Albergaria et al., 2016).  Its biological properties make it a versatile organism to use in mass production, especially in the fields of health and food science (Maneira et al., 2025, Albergaria et al., 2016).  In the context of medicine, for example, *S. cerevisiae* has been used to experiment with a few different health-related applications as a “cell factory”, including in biopharmaceuticals (ex. recombinant insulin synthesis), vaccines (for immunogen production or as a carrier), and yeast-based biosensors (Maneira et al., 2025).  Meanwhile, in the food industry, it is much more greatly appreciated and enjoyed in baking and brewing, for its greater fitness to perform alcoholic fermentation under strong environmental stress conditions (Albergaria et al., 2016).  This fermentation, which sees most sugars being converted into ethanol, is usually a very critical step for the quality production of most foods, including bread, fruit, and wine (Albergaria et al., 2016).  For this reason, researchers have often studied this eukaryotic species using a variety of methods for transcriptome analysis, including RNAseq, to understand the profiles of genes and proteins behind its various cellular mechanisms and processes (Mardonov et al., 2020).

## Methods
The raw data for the *Saccharomyces cerevisiae* I-329 strain originated from the study conducted by Kishkovskaia et al., which were then investigated in the bulk transcriptomics experiment conducted by Mardanov et al. at three different experimental stages of Velum biofilm: early, thin, and mature (2017, 2020).  The transcriptome sequences for the three replicates investigated at each stage was obtained from NCBI, for a total of 9 different samples (Mardanov et al., 2020, Kishkovskaia et al., 2019, Institute of Bioengineering, Research Center of Biotechnology of RAS, 2019).  Data for the reference transcriptome, including raw sequences and annotation data, were obtained from the R16 genome assembly of *S. cerevisiae* strain S288C on the NCBI RefSeq database (Saccharomyces Genome Database, 2014).  Transcript-level expression and abundance of each replicate was then quantified using quasi-mapping with Salmon v1.10.3, and the Salmon index was constructed from the RNA transcript sequences of the S288C strain (Patro et al., 2017).

Differential expression analysis was conducted on the quantified replicates in R (v4.5.2) using the DESeq2 pseudoaligner (v1.50.2) (Love et al., 2014, Rdocumentation, n.d.).  The differences in gene expression were evaluated statistically using pairwise comparisons between experimental groups according to the multiple-comparison adjusted p-values (padj), using the early biofilm as the primary reference (thin vs. early & mature vs. early) and the thin biofilm as the secondary reference (mature vs. thin).  Data structure was assessed using principal component analysis (PCA) for dimensional reduction to explore key sources of variance and determine their size (% variance).  For other visualizations (heat map, MA plot, volcano plot per comparison), shrinkage was also applied to the log2FoldChange (LFC) of genes for each comparison using the ashr shrinkage method (Stephens, 2017).  In particular, for the heatmaps, the top 20 genes were selected based on p-value for visualization.  Lastly, functional annotation and comparison was also conducted in R using the clusterProfiler (v4.18.4), enrichplot (v1.30.4), and org.Sc.sgd.db (v3.22.0) packages (Xu et al., 2024, Yu et al., 2012, Carlson, 2025).  The genes were ranked in order by Wald statistic and analyzed using Gene Ontology (GO)-enriched Fast Gene Set Enrichment Analysis (FGSEA) by biological processes, with a p-value cutoff of 0.05 (Ashburner et al., 2000, The Gene Ontology Consortium, 2025, Thomas et al., 2021, Korotkevich et al., 2016).  Upregulated and downregulated gene sets were compared and visualized separately using dot plots and ridge plots.

## Results
### Data structure
To ensure that interpretations of gene expression differences between developmental stages of Velum were accurate, the data structure of the *S. cerevisiae* I-329 replicates was first evaluated for any major overlaps.  Upon applying PCA analysis for dimensional reduction (Figure 1), the two largest sources of variation appeared to be the developmental stage of Velum and potentially structural differences in transcriptome sequences between samples, though this exact factor was unclear.

**Figure 1**: PCA plot of *S. cerivisiae* transcriptome data.  Velum biofilm stage (PC1) explained 67% of the variance, while sample structure (PC2) likely explained 26% of the variance.  The replicates were clustered according to the developmental stages of Velum biofilm that they were observed. 

Taking a closer look at Figure 1, the replicates appeared to form three distinct clusters with each other, in line with the three stages of Velum development.  The developmental stage (PC1) appeared explain 67% of the variance, while sequence structure (PC2) likely explained 26% of the variance, for a total of 93% variance explained by these components.  The clusters for early and mature biofilm replicates appeared to be more tightly packed.  However, there was some variance/spread in sequence structure for the replicates from the thin biofilm, though not necessarily large enough to warrant concerns of error due to overlap.  This suggested that the replicates were sufficiently differentiated enough between developmental stages of Velum, though they also remained mostly similar to each other in terms of sequence structure, allowing for accurate analysis of differential gene expression.

### Functional annotation and GO-GSEA comparison
Genes were functionally annotated using Gene Ontology by Biological Processes (GO-BP) and compared using Fast Gene Set Enrichment Analysis (FGSEA).  This helped to gain a clearer understanding of the changes in upregulation or downregulation of essential biological functions during Velum formation.  When looking at the different dot plots in Figure 5, the evidence showed notable differences in expression levels, whether activated or suppressed, between the pairwise comparisons.

**Figure 5**: Dot plots of Gene Ontology (GO) enrichment terms for Fast Gene Set Enrichment Analysis (FGSEA) for each pairwise comparison: **(a)** thin and early stages, **(b)** mature and early stages, and **(c)** mature and thin stages.  The genes were ranked based on Wald statistic and categorized by Biological Process (BP) ontology.  Each plot is further split into two separate categories to distinguish expression level of terms: activated (for upregulation) and suppressed (for downregulation), relative to the primary (early biofilm) or secondary (thin biofilm) reference.  Dot size reflects the number of genes identified for each term, while dot colour represents the statistical significance according to adjusted p-values, with a cutoff of *padj* < 0.05.  Larger dot coloured in red for a BP term suggest stronger evidence of greater differential expression.

First, comparing the thin biofilm replicates to the early biofilm (Figure 5a), there were notably multiple GO terms that indicated high statistical significance during upregulation.  The upregulated functions containing the largest gene sets out of these terms were translation, including both cytoplasmic and mitochondrial translation; ribosomal biogenesis, including that of large and small subunits; rRNA processing and its metabolic processes; and mitochondrial gene expression.  On the other hand, the most statistically significantly downregulated function in thin biofilm compared to early biofilm was the lipid metabolic process.

Next, comparing the mature biofilm to the early biofilm (Figure 5b), the number of upregulated GO terms with large gene sets appeared to be much less than the thin biofilm.  This time, the mature Velum biofilm appeared to place more emphasis on upregulating important mitochondrial functions, including mitochondrial translation; mitochondrial gene expression; and mitochondrial organization.  In addition to these, translation and cytoplasmic translation also continued to remain upregulated with somewhat large gene sets, much like the thin biofilm.  The lipid metabolic process, along with the lipid biosynthetic process, continued to remain as the highest statistically significant downregulated functions with the largest gene sets.

Lastly, comparing the mature biofilm to the thin biofilm (Figure 5c), the number of GO terms with higher gene sets appeared to be slightly lower for this pairwise comparison as opposed to their comparisons with the early biofilm.  In addition, there were not as many terms that exhibited a higher degree of statistical significance.  For the upregulated functions, the ones with the highest gene sets placed more focus on the metabolic and biosynthetic processes for glycogen; the metabolic processes for glucan and energy reserves; and many catabolic pathways, which included macromolecules, proteins, ubiquitin-dependent pathways, and proteasome-mediated pathways.  The downregulated functions that showed the highest statistical significance and the largest gene sets this time were the lipid biosynthetic process, along with transmembrane transport.

All these observations of the GO-GSEA comparison were then further validated using ridge plots to investigate their distributions more closely to determine the most consistent upregulations and downregulations across different groups (Figure 6a).  For thin and early biofilms (Figure 6a), most of the upregulated functions, with a Wald statistic above 0, such as translation, appeared to show normal distributions.  However, there were others, such as ribosomal biogenesis, that had a thin tail to the right, indicating the presence of right-skewedness.  Among the terms that were downregulated, as evidenced by their positioning with a Wald statistic below 0, the lipid metabolic process carried the most statistical significance, though its distribution did exhibit a thin tail to the left, indicating left-skewedness.  For mature and early biofilms (Figure 6b), the mitochondrial functions all expressed normal distributions, further cementing them as key characteristics of the mature biofilm.  However, the ridge plot also appeared to identify protein folding as another upregulated GO term during the mature biofilm, though it was not quite as statistically significant as the mitochondrial functions.  Meanwhile, the lipid metabolic process and lipid biosynthesis continued to be the most downregulated terms, with its ridges appearing to be more normally distributed.  Finally, for the mature and thin biofilm comparison (Figure 6c), the distributions for upregulated functions were mostly normal for catabolic processes, though the metabolic processes for glycogen, glucan, and energy reserves appeared to show slightly more bimodal distributions.  Altogether, the functional annotation and comparison with GO-GSEA proved highly instrumental in clearly identifying the main biological functions of each developmental stage and their changes in differential expression levels.
